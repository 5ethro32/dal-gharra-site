<article class="article">

<p class="lede reveal">
    Every previous technological revolution followed the same script. Machines replaced what humans did with their hands, and humans moved to what they could do with their minds. The spinning jenny destroyed weaving jobs and created factory jobs. The spreadsheet destroyed bookkeeping jobs and created analyst jobs. The pattern held for two hundred years. And then, in the space of about eighteen months, it stopped holding.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    This isn't a speculative argument about what artificial intelligence might do one day. It's an examination of what is already visible in the data, if you know where to look. The numbers are not dramatic in the way a financial crisis is dramatic. There is no single moment of collapse. Instead, there is a slow, structural divergence between economic output and human employment that is now too large to explain away as a blip. The data in this piece is primarily American — it's where the labour market statistics are most granular and where AI capital expenditure is concentrated — but the companies doing the disrupting operate globally. When software seats get cut, they get cut in Edinburgh and London too.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    The case against jobs is not that everyone will be unemployed. It's that the mechanism which has reliably created new employment after every previous technological disruption — the link between productivity and hiring — appears to be breaking. And the economists, executives, and policymakers best positioned to see this are watching headline unemployment and GDP, when the signal is in the ratio between the two: in labour share, in white-collar employment by sector, in the growing gap between output and hours worked.
</p>

<h2 class="reveal">The numbers that don't add up</h2>

<p class="reveal">
    In February 2026, the Bureau of Labor Statistics quietly revised its estimate of total US job creation for 2025. The original figure — 584,000 jobs — was revised down to just 181,000. This was not a rounding error. It was the largest downward revision in recent memory, and it told a story that the original data had obscured.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    During the same period, GDP grew at 2.7%. The economy expanded. Corporate profits hit record highs. The Atlanta Fed's GDPNow model tracked Q4 2025 growth at 3.7%, and some forecasters projected 2026 could reach as high as 6%. None of this growth was accompanied by proportionate hiring.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    Goldman Sachs now estimates that underlying trend job growth has fallen to 11,000 per month — below even the modest breakeven rate needed to hold unemployment steady. Their analysts describe a plausible scenario of "jobless growth similar to the jobless recovery of the early 2000s."
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    Oxford Economics expects job gains to average less than 40,000 per month during 2026, characterising the labour market as a "low-hire, low-fire" equilibrium. The Fed's Beige Book noted that "a few firms reported that artificial intelligence replaced entry-level positions or made existing workers productive enough to curb new hiring."
</p>

<h2 class="reveal">The productivity J-curve</h2>

<p class="reveal">
    Erik Brynjolfsson, director of the Stanford Digital Economy Lab, has been studying the relationship between AI and productivity longer than almost anyone. In a widely-cited Financial Times op-ed published in February 2026, he argued that the data now supports what he calls the "productivity J-curve" — a pattern in which general-purpose technologies suppress measurable productivity during an initial investment phase before entering a harvest phase where gains become visible.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    His analysis suggests US productivity jumped roughly 2.7% in 2025 — nearly double the 1.4% annual average over the past decade. The mechanism is straightforward: GDP rose while hours worked barely moved. "The updated 2025 US data suggests we are now transitioning out of this investment phase into a harvest phase where those earlier efforts begin to manifest as measurable output," he wrote.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    This is the optimistic reading: productivity is genuinely increasing, and companies are learning to do more with less. But "doing more with less" is a phrase that sounds very different depending on whether you're a shareholder or an employee.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    Brynjolfsson himself acknowledges the duality. While most businesses still use AI for narrow tasks — translation, summarisation, drafting — he has found "a small cohort of power users" who are automating end-to-end workstreams with AI agents, compressing weeks of work into hours. The gap between these two groups is widening rapidly. The question is what happens when the power-user approach becomes the default.
</p>

<div class="evidence-card reveal">
    <div class="ev-header">The Burning Glass Institute Data</div>
    <p>
        Gad Levanon, chief economist at the Burning Glass Institute, has tracked white-collar employment specifically. His data shows a clear structural break: employment in finance, insurance, information, and professional services peaked in November 2022 — the same month ChatGPT launched — then edged down, even as real GDP in those sectors continued to rise and accelerate.
    </p>
    <p>
        "AI-enabled automation is therefore a plausible contributor, even if the data cannot isolate its specific role," he wrote. The timing is unlikely to be coincidental.
    </p>
</div>

<p class="reveal">
    A fair objection: correlation is not causation. And the strongest alternative explanation deserves stating plainly. November 2022 wasn't just the month ChatGPT launched — it was deep into the Federal Reserve's most aggressive tightening cycle in forty years. Interest rates went from near-zero to over 5% between 2022 and 2024. That alone suppresses hiring, particularly in white-collar professional services, regardless of AI. The BLS revision could reflect methodological lag rather than structural change. The productivity jump could be post-pandemic normalisation. The SaaS repricing could be a valuation correction after a decade of cheap capital.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    I don't think those explanations are sufficient, but I want to be honest about why. Monetary tightening explains a hiring slowdown. It doesn't explain why GDP accelerated through the same period, or why productivity nearly doubled its decade average, or why the stock market wiped a trillion dollars from software companies on the explicit thesis that AI replaces human workers. Each datapoint has an innocent explanation. But the innocent explanations require multiple unrelated causes to converge simultaneously — tight money causing hiring weakness, pandemic recovery causing the productivity spike, and unrelated sentiment causing the SaaS collapse — while a single cause connects all three. That doesn't prove the AI explanation is correct. But it's the more parsimonious one, and the capital flows suggest the market agrees.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    That's the case this piece is making: not proof, but a pattern too coherent to dismiss. A bet, not a verdict.
</p>

    <span class="ornament"></span>
    <span class="numeral">III</span>
    <span class="ornament"></span>
</div>

<h2 class="reveal">The trillion-dollar repricing</h2>

<p class="reveal">
    If the labour market data is a slow, structural signal, the stock market is the loud one. In the first eight weeks of 2026, more than $1 trillion in market capitalisation was wiped from the software sector. The S&P 500 Software & Services Index fell roughly 20% year-to-date. Salesforce dropped 33%. ServiceNow fell 36%. HubSpot collapsed 51%. Monday.com lost 44%.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    The sell-off was triggered by a cascade of events in early February. Palantir CEO Alex Karp told investors that AI had become so powerful at writing and managing enterprise software that many SaaS companies were in danger of becoming irrelevant. Anthropic released Claude Cowork, a suite of autonomous agents capable of managing multi-step business processes without human intervention. The Nasdaq Cloud Index lost nearly $300 billion in a single 48-hour window.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    The core thesis driving the sell-off is what analysts call "seat compression." The traditional SaaS business model charges per user — per seat. If one AI agent can perform the work that previously required a dozen human "seats," the revenue model collapses. Companies that once required 500 software licenses for a department found they could achieve the same output with 50, by deploying autonomous agents that work around the clock without a user interface.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    This is not hypothetical. An unverified but widely-circulated memo from a Fortune 50 company outlined plans to reduce its Salesforce and ServiceNow license spend by 60% by year-end, replacing human-operated software with raw API credits from AI providers. The sourcing is thin — leaked, unnamed — but it aligns with what enterprise software analysts are hearing privately. The comment section on Wes Roth's "$1 Trillion Gone" video tells the story at the individual level. One commenter wrote: "DocuSign wanted a fortune from our marketing team to e-sign 1 million cases. We replaced the technology in 2 hours."
</p>

    <span class="ornament"></span>
</div>

<h2 class="reveal">The case for calm</h2>

<div class="counter-section reveal">
    <h3>The other side</h3>
    <p>
        The counterarguments are not weak.
    </p>
    <p>
        <strong>Wedbush Securities</strong> argues that enterprises won't rip out tens of billions of dollars of existing software infrastructure to migrate to AI providers. Large organisations took decades to accumulate trillions of data points ingrained in their current systems. Migration is not a quarterly decision — it's a multi-year programme with real switching costs and integration risk. The SaaS incumbents may lose their growth premium without losing their revenue base.
    </p>
    <p>
        <strong>The Klarna reversal</strong> is the most prominent real-world cautionary tale. In 2024, Klarna replaced 700 customer service agents with AI, claiming the technology handled 75% of customer chats — 2.3 million conversations a month. By mid-2025, CEO Sebastian Siemiatkowski admitted they "went too far." Customer satisfaction dropped, quality deteriorated on complex cases, and the company began rehiring. AI handled the volume but broke on nuance, empathy, and the edge cases that actually define customer relationships. The gap between demo and deployment remains wide.
    </p>
    <p>
        <strong>The Yale Budget Lab</strong> found that November and December 2025 CPS data "do not suggest a substantial change in AI's impact on the labour market." Of roughly 1.2 million total layoffs in 2025, only 55,000 were attributed to AI — about 4.5%. The vast majority of job losses still come from the usual suspects: restructuring, demand shifts, and cost-cutting unrelated to technology. If AI were truly eating employment, you'd expect the layoff data to show it by now.
    </p>
    <p>
        And Salesforce, for all its stock price carnage, still expects 12% revenue growth this year. ServiceNow's backlog is at record levels. The market is pricing in a future that hasn't arrived in the income statements. Stock fear and operational reality are telling different stories — and historically, the income statement wins.
    </p>
</div>

<p class="reveal">
    These points deserve weight. The bearish case rests on a pattern that is visible but not yet conclusive. The bullish case rests on two centuries of historical precedent and the reasonable observation that technology transitions take longer than forecast and create more opportunities than anyone predicts.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    But here's where I think the optimists have a blind spot.
</p>

    <span class="ornament"></span>
    <span class="numeral">V</span>
    <span class="ornament"></span>
</div>

<h2 class="reveal">Why this revolution may break the pattern</h2>

<p class="reveal">
    Every previous industrial revolution automated what humans did with their bodies and created work for what humans did with their minds. The spinning jenny automated weaving, but created factory management. The assembly line automated fabrication, but created engineering and logistics. Computers automated arithmetic, but created programming and analysis. In each case, the new work required cognitive skills the machines couldn't match.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    This revolution automates cognitive work itself. And that puts the escape route in question for the first time.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    It's worth acknowledging the weight of the counterargument here. Every generation has believed its technology was the one that would finally break the employment pattern. Economists in the 1960s were certain that automation would create permanent mass unemployment. They were wrong. The pattern held because new categories of work emerged that nobody predicted — the internet didn't just destroy travel agents, it created an entire gig economy, social media industry, and app development ecosystem that would have been unimaginable in 1995.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    But there is a structural difference worth taking seriously. The Burning Glass data from section II illustrates the mechanism: the sectors shedding workers aren't manufacturing or logistics — they're finance, insurance, information, and professional services. These are precisely the cognitive-work categories that absorbed displaced workers in every previous transition. When the destination itself is being automated, the historical playbook may not apply. That doesn't mean no new categories will emerge. It means the burden of proof has shifted. The optimistic case now requires identifying what kind of work humans will do that AI cannot — and so far, the answers are vague.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    The investment figures confirm the direction. Global AI spending is forecast to reach $2.52 trillion in 2026, a 44% increase year-over-year. The five largest US cloud and AI infrastructure providers — Microsoft, Alphabet, Amazon, Meta, and Oracle — have committed between $660 billion and $690 billion in capital expenditure for 2026 alone, nearly doubling their 2025 levels. Between 2013 and 2024, total global corporate investment in AI reached $1.6 trillion, surpassing the cost of the Apollo programme, the Interstate Highway System, and the Manhattan Project combined.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    Companies do not spend $2.5 trillion on "maybe."
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    Microsoft's AI chief Mustafa Suleyman has put a timeline on it: eighteen months before "anyone looking at a computer for a living" is materially affected. He may be wrong on the timeline — executives selling transformative technology have obvious incentives to overstate its impact. But the capital allocation says the intent is real, and it is being deployed at a scale that dwarfs any previous corporate technology investment.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    And the gains aren't distributing evenly. The Bank of America Institute describes a "K-shaped" divergence that is "beginning to look more like the jaws of a crocodile." Asset holders prosper — corporate profits hit $3.59 trillion annualised in Q3 2025. Wage earners stagnate — real disposable income was literally flat in the same quarter. Labour's share of global GDP has fallen to 52.4%, continuing a decline since the 1980s. In the US, it's at its lowest since the Great Depression.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    The headline GDP number combines both groups. The lived economy does not.
</p>

    <span class="ornament"></span>
</div>

<h2 class="reveal">The bet</h2>

<p class="reveal">
    The argument is not that the sky is falling. It's that the mechanism which has reliably turned technological disruption into net job creation — the shift from manual to cognitive work — has no obvious successor when cognitive work itself gets automated.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    The economy is generating wealth. The question is whether the mechanism for distributing that wealth — employment — can survive what's being built to replace it. Moody's Mark Zandi put it plainly: "Either productivity booms, creating more job losses, or investors are wrong about the potential of AI, potentially triggering a market correction. There's risks on both sides of this fragile growth."
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    The scorecard below is the bet. I'll update it when the data moves. If I'm wrong, the numbers will show it. But if I'm right, the question that follows is harder than anything in this essay: what does an economy look like when it no longer needs most of the people living in it to function?
</p>
