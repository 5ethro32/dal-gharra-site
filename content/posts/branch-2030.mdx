## The problem is not footfall

Every year the conversation returns to the same question: are bank branches dying? The data says footfall is falling. The cost-per-transaction is rising. Digital adoption is accelerating. And yet, when you close a branch, the backlash is fierce and deeply personal.

The mistake is framing this as a binary. Branches are not dying. They are trapped in a model designed for a world that no longer exists — one where the majority of interactions were transactional, and the branch was the only channel available.

## Four pressures converging

The branch faces four forces simultaneously, and none of them are going away.

**Cost pressure.** A simple in-branch transaction costs roughly fifty times what the same transaction costs digitally. When 85% of routine banking can be handled through an app, the economics of maintaining a full-service branch network become increasingly difficult to justify.

**Regulatory pressure.** Consumer Duty demands that firms deliver good outcomes for customers. This is not about compliance paperwork — it is about demonstrating that the advice customers receive in branches is genuinely in their interest, documented, and consistent. The bar has been raised.

**Customer expectation pressure.** People who bank digitally six days a week still want a branch for the moments that matter — a mortgage conversation, a bereavement, a business loan. Their tolerance for queuing to pay in a cheque has disappeared, but their need for human expertise has not.

**Technology pressure.** Generative AI is not a future technology. It is here, it is improving rapidly, and it is already reshaping how knowledge work gets done in every sector. Financial services will not be the exception.

## What the branch becomes

The branch of 2030 is not a smaller version of today's branch. It is a fundamentally different space with a different purpose.

**From transactional to advisory.** The majority of routine transactions move to digital and assisted self-service. The branch becomes a space for complex, high-value conversations — mortgages, business banking, wealth planning, vulnerability support.

**From generalist to specialist.** Branch colleagues are no longer expected to know everything about every product. AI-powered knowledge systems provide real-time support, surfacing relevant information, regulatory guidance, and product details during customer conversations.

**From reactive to proactive.** Instead of waiting for customers to walk in with a problem, branches use data to identify customers who would benefit from a conversation. Outbound engagement replaces passive availability.

**From isolated to connected.** The branch becomes one node in an integrated channel strategy. A customer starts a mortgage conversation online, continues it in-branch, and completes documentation digitally — with full continuity across every touchpoint.

## The role of GenAI

Generative AI is not the entire answer, but it is what makes the shift possible at scale.

**Real-time colleague support.** During a customer conversation, AI can surface product information, regulatory requirements, and previous interaction history — allowing the colleague to focus entirely on the customer rather than navigating multiple systems.

**Automated documentation.** Compliance documentation, meeting notes, and outcome recording can be generated and structured automatically, reducing the administrative burden that currently consumes a significant portion of branch colleagues' time.

**Consistent quality.** AI-assisted guidance helps ensure that the advice given in a Glasgow branch on a Tuesday afternoon meets the same standard as the advice given in a London branch on a Thursday morning. Consumer Duty demands consistency. AI enables it.

**Training and development.** New colleagues can be supported by AI coaching tools that help them develop advisory skills faster, with scenario-based practice and real-time feedback.

## What this requires

Technology alone does not transform a branch network. The shift requires investment in three areas simultaneously.

**People.** Branch colleagues need different skills — advisory capability, emotional intelligence, complex problem-solving. This means sustained investment in training and development, not a one-off programme.

**Process.** Operating models need to change. Appointment-based advisory models, flexible staffing, and integrated digital-physical workflows replace the traditional counter-service approach.

**Culture.** The hardest part. Moving from a culture that measures success by transactions processed to one that measures success by outcomes delivered. This is a leadership challenge as much as a technology challenge.

## The opportunity

The bank that gets this right does not just reduce costs. It builds something genuinely valuable — a branch network that customers choose to visit because the experience is worth their time, staffed by people who are empowered to have the conversations that matter, supported by technology that handles everything else.

The branch is not dying. It is waiting to be reimagined.
